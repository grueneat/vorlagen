# Human-Centered Design Principles for Print Political Communication

A working guide for materials produced for Bündnis 90/Die Grünen candidates (flyers, falzflyer, posters, leaflets, party publications). Every principle is tagged to one or both of the two judgment axes the user has set:

- **A** — does it appeal to humans
- **T** — does it transport information well

Each entry lists the source, the axis, an actionable rule, and a worked example or anti-example.

---

# Principles for human appeal

## 1. Compose around one dominant element

**Source:** Paul Rand, *A Designer's Art* (1985); Saul Bass posters and title sequences (1955–1990); restated in Ellen Lupton, *Graphic Design: The New Basics* (2008).
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** Each panel of a falzflyer and each poster has exactly one element that owns >40% of the optical weight. That element is decided first; everything else is sized and placed in relation to it.
**Worked example:** On a Kandidat-Falzflyer cover, the candidate portrait is the dominant element at ~55% of the panel area. The logo, name, and slogan are deliberately scaled smaller so the eye lands on the face first, then resolves the identity.
**Anti-example:** Logo top-left at 12%, portrait at 35%, headline at 25%, slogan at 18% — four elements competing for attention, none winning. The viewer's eye scans without resting; the panel feels "busy but empty."

## 2. Asymmetric balance over centered symmetry

**Source:** Jan Tschichold, *Die neue Typographie* (1928); also explicit in Müller-Brockmann's later grid work.
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** Default to asymmetric layouts that balance optical weight across an axis, not literal centering. Centered layouts read as formal/civic-bureaucratic; asymmetric reads as confident and modern, which fits Grüne brand positioning.
**Worked example:** Headline left-aligned to column 1–4 of an 8-column grid; portrait right-aligned to column 5–8, slightly heavier than the type block. The two balance optically without mirroring.
**Anti-example:** Headline centered, candidate name centered below, portrait centered below that, logo centered at the bottom. Reads as a tombstone or wedding invitation, not a campaign.

## 3. Generous white space signals confidence

**Source:** Josef Müller-Brockmann, *Grid Systems in Graphic Design* (1981); Massimo Vignelli, *The Vignelli Canon* (2008, p. 38–42 on "the discipline").
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** Reserve at least 30% of every panel as intentional negative space (margins + interior whitespace). Crowded panels signal amateur production and visual anxiety; spacious panels signal that the message has authority and doesn't need to shout.
**Worked example:** A poster with the slogan "Mehr drin." on the upper third, 60% of the lower area left as flat green field, candidate portrait small on the bottom right. The whitespace is the design.
**Anti-example:** A flyer panel with text running to all four edges, no margin, multiple boxes and rules dividing the space — reads as a discount supermarket leaflet.

## 4. Faces are the strongest attention magnet

**Source:** Eye-tracking studies — Alfred Yarbus, *Eye Movements and Vision* (1967); Itti & Koch saliency models (2001); James Breeze "How Do Eyes Follow Faces" (Usable World, replicated in Nielsen Norman Group studies, 2006–2017).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Place a high-quality candidate portrait early in the gaze path (top-left or upper-third for left-to-right readers). Where the candidate is looking, the viewer's eye will follow within ~200ms — orient the candidate's gaze toward the headline or CTA, never off the page.
**Worked example:** Portrait upper-left, candidate gazing slightly right and down toward the headline "Für ein lebenswertes Wien." The headline inherits the attention transferred from the face.
**Anti-example:** Portrait upper-right with candidate gazing further right (off the page edge). The viewer's attention follows the gaze and exits the design.

## 5. Limit the type palette to two families, three sizes per panel

**Source:** Massimo Vignelli, *The Vignelli Canon* (2008); confirmed in Bringhurst, *The Elements of Typographic Style* (1992, §1.3.4 on type harmony).
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** Per panel use at most one display family + one text family, and at most three distinct type sizes. Beyond that, the page reads as "ransom note" composition.
**Worked example:** Headline in PT Sans Bold 48pt; subhead in PT Sans Regular 18pt; body in PT Serif Regular 10pt. Three sizes, two families, total typographic discipline.
**Anti-example:** Five different sizes between 9pt and 32pt mixing three families because "we wanted variety" — produces visual fragmentation and signals lack of authorial confidence.

## 6. Color carries political meaning before it carries aesthetic meaning

**Source:** Eva Heller, *Wie Farben wirken* (1989, expanded 2000) — empirical color-association study with 1,888 German respondents; also Frank Brettschneider on political color semantics in *Politische Kommunikation in der Mediengesellschaft* (2002).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** In German political print, color is a party signature before it is decoration. Greens own grass-green and dark-green; do not flirt with red (SPD), blue (AfD/CDU), magenta (Linke), or yellow-saturated (FDP) as primary colors. Warm accents (deep orange, ochre) read as "Greens with warmth"; cool accents (teal, mint) read as "Greens with environmental seriousness." Avoid more than two non-green accent colors per piece.
**Worked example:** A flyer using Bund Hellgrün (#46962B) as primary, Dunkelgrün (#1B5E20) for body emphasis, and a single warm ochre as accent for CTA buttons. Three colors, immediately legible as Grüne.
**Anti-example:** A flyer with green, red, and blue stripes "for visual interest" — reads as ambiguous or even centrist-confused, dilutes the brand cue.

## 7. Photography style consistency outranks photography quality

**Source:** Paul Rand, *Thoughts on Design* (1947); Erik Spiekermann, *Stop Stealing Sheep & Find Out How Type Works* (1993, on coherence).
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** All photographs in a single piece must share treatment — same lighting temperature, same depth-of-field range, same crop philosophy (eye-level vs. low-angle), same color grade. A mediocre but consistent set of photos beats a mix of one professional shot and three smartphone snapshots.
**Worked example:** Five candidate photos, all natural daylight, all eye-level, all with shallow background blur, all desaturated by ~10% — feels like one campaign.
**Anti-example:** Cover photo studio-lit, interior photos flash-on-camera, group photo in fluorescent indoor light. Reads as pasted together.

## 8. Faces should fill at least 60% of their crop

**Source:** Steve McCurry composition rules (paraphrased in *The Iconic Photographs*, 2012); Annie Leibovitz portrait technique.
**Axis:** A
**Rule:** A candidate portrait used as a primary element must crop tight — eyes in the upper third of the frame, head + shoulders only, no wide environmental shots for primary use. Wide shots may appear as secondary supporting images.
**Worked example:** Portrait cropped from forehead-top to mid-chest, eyes at one-third line, slight rule-of-thirds offset. Feels intimate and decisive.
**Anti-example:** Full-body shot of the candidate at a podium taking 25% of the frame, surrounded by an empty conference hall — eyes are 12 pixels tall, viewer cannot connect.

# Principles for information transport

## 9. Headlines: maximum 7 words, readable in 3 seconds

**Source:** David Ogilvy, *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963); replicated in outdoor-advertising readability research (OAAA *Communicating Outdoors*, 2010); also Dieter Rams' "Ten Principles" point 4 (makes a product understandable).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Cover/poster headlines never exceed 7 words and ~35 characters. The headline must be readable and parseable inside the 1.5–3 seconds a passerby spends on a poster or a doorstep flyer.
**Worked example:** "Wien für alle. Bezahlbar wohnen." — 5 words, two clauses, parses instantly.
**Anti-example:** "Gemeinsam gestalten wir ein nachhaltiges, gerechtes und lebenswertes Wien für die nächsten Generationen." — 13 words; nobody finishes reading it before walking past.

## 10. Hierarchy via type contrast, not type variety

**Source:** Robert Bringhurst, *The Elements of Typographic Style* (1992, §3.1); Ellen Lupton, *Thinking with Type* (2010, §2 "Text").
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Achieve hierarchy through one strong size jump (≥2.5×) between headline and body, plus weight contrast (regular vs. bold). Do not invent hierarchy by introducing new fonts or colors — that confuses rather than clarifies.
**Worked example:** Headline 48pt bold, body 14pt regular, both in the same family. The 3.4× ratio creates unambiguous hierarchy with one design decision.
**Anti-example:** Headline 24pt italic in Family A, subhead 22pt bold in Family B, body 14pt regular in Family C. The 24/22 jump is too small to register as hierarchy; the family changes add noise without signal.

## 11. Body text: 45–75 characters per line

**Source:** Bringhurst, *Elements of Typographic Style* (1992, §2.1.2): "Anything from 45 to 75 characters is widely regarded as a satisfactory length of line for a single-column page set in a serifed text face… 66 characters is widely regarded as ideal."
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Set body columns to land between 45 and 75 characters per line. On a DIN-lang falzflyer panel (~99mm wide), this typically means body around 9–10pt with ~22–28mm side margins.
**Worked example:** Two-column body grid on the inside of a falzflyer, each column ~62 characters wide, 10pt PT Serif at 13pt leading — comfortable reading rhythm.
**Anti-example:** A single full-bleed column 100mm wide at 11pt — yields ~95 characters per line; the eye loses its line return and skips lines.

## 12. Minimum font sizes for older voters

**Source:** WCAG 2.2 (W3C, 2023) for digital, mapped to print by Tiresias / RNIB *Clear Print Guidelines* (2007); Designing for the Third Age (Coleman, 2001).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Body text in print never below 10pt; 11pt preferred. Captions/legal never below 8pt. Sans-serif with open counters (PT Sans, Source Sans, Lato) for anyone over 60. Grüne demographic skews younger; print materials need to work harder to reach 60+ readers, so err generous.
**Worked example:** Body 11pt PT Sans at 14pt leading; captions 9pt; legal/Impressum 8pt. Reading at arm's length is comfortable for a 65-year-old without readers.
**Anti-example:** 8pt body "to fit more content" — half the over-60 audience cannot read it without lifting the flyer to their nose, which they will not do.

## 13. Contrast ratios at WCAG AA minimum, AAA preferred

**Source:** W3C WCAG 2.2 (2023), success criterion 1.4.3 (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text); ISO 9241-303 ergonomic principles.
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Body text on background must hit a 4.5:1 luminance contrast minimum, 7:1 preferred. Light-green-on-white (Bund Hellgrün #46962B on white) measures ~4.7:1 — borderline; switch to Dunkelgrün (~9.5:1) for body and reserve Hellgrün for display, panels, and accents.
**Worked example:** Body text Dunkelgrün on white = 9.5:1, AAA. Headlines Hellgrün on Dunkelgrün panel = 4.0:1, acceptable for ≥18pt display only.
**Anti-example:** Light-grey body text on light-green panel — measures 2.1:1, fails AA, illegible to anyone with mild vision impairment (which is roughly 15–20% of the over-50 population).

## 14. One CTA per panel, unmissable

**Source:** Steve Krug, *Don't Make Me Think* (2000) — translated to print by direct-mail research summarized in Ogilvy & Mather *How to Create Advertising That Sells* (1982).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Every panel that has an action — vote, attend, RSVP, donate, scan QR — has exactly one such action, visually distinct from body content (color block, button shape, or whitespace isolation), placed near the natural exit point of the gaze path (lower-right for Latin-script readers).
**Worked example:** Back panel of a falzflyer: "Am 10. Mai wählen." in 24pt with the date in an orange chip; QR code right next to it; nothing else competes.
**Anti-example:** Three CTAs on one panel: "Spenden", "Mitmachen", "Folgen" — each gets ⅓ of the attention, none gets acted on.

## 15. Maximize information-ink, minimize chartjunk

**Source:** Edward Tufte, *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (1983, ch. 4 on data-ink ratio; ch. 5 on chartjunk).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Every graphic element on a flyer must carry information. Decorative gradients, drop shadows, 3D effects, fake-tape, fake-staple, and ornamental rules are chartjunk — remove them. If a rule, box, or icon does not change what the reader knows, it does not belong.
**Worked example:** A statistic "47% mehr Radwege" set in 60pt Hellgrün next to one icon of a bicycle. Two elements, both informational.
**Anti-example:** The same statistic inside a 3D rounded-corner box with gradient fill, drop shadow, decorative leaf-vine border, and a "since 2019!" banner ribbon. Eight elements, one piece of information, six pieces of noise.

## 16. Layered information density (skim layer + read layer)

**Source:** Edward Tufte, *Envisioning Information* (1990), ch. 2 "Layering and Separation"; also newspaper design — Mario Garcia & Pegie Stark, *Eyes on the News* (1991).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Design two readable depths: a skim layer (headline + pull quote + one statistic) understandable in 3 seconds, and a read layer (body, details, sources) for the reader who has stopped to engage. The skim layer must function alone; the read layer must reward investment without being required.
**Worked example:** Inside spread of a falzflyer with three large theme cards (each: 2-word title + 1-line claim) as the skim layer, and 4-line body paragraphs underneath as the read layer.
**Anti-example:** Wall-of-text panel, one font size, no entry points — the 3-second reader gets nothing and walks on.

## 17. Reading order follows a deliberate gaze path

**Source:** Mario Garcia & Pegie Stark, *Eyes on the News* (Poynter, 1991); replicated in EyeTrack 2007 (Poynter); James Breeze (2009) on F- and Z-pattern in static layouts.
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** Decide the reading order before placing elements. For posters and covers: dominant element → headline → identifier (logo/name) → CTA. For text-heavy panels: top-left entry, F-pattern scan, exit lower-right. Place the CTA at the exit point, not the entry.
**Worked example:** Falzflyer cover gaze path designed as: portrait (top-left) → headline (right of portrait) → name (under portrait) → "Wahltag 10. Mai" CTA (bottom-right). Path is a clean diagonal.
**Anti-example:** CTA top-left, headline bottom-right, portrait middle — viewer's eye ricochets and gives up before parsing.

## 18. Small multiples for comparison, never standalone bars

**Source:** Edward Tufte, *Envisioning Information* (1990), ch. 4 "Small Multiples"; also Tufte (1983).
**Axis:** T
**Rule:** When comparing positions, years, or districts, use small multiples — repeating the same chart shape with one varying dimension. Avoid single bar charts on a flyer; they read as bureaucratic and rarely communicate the point as cleanly as 3 side-by-side icons or mini-charts.
**Worked example:** Three identical horizontal bars showing "Mietpreis 2015 / 2020 / 2025" stacked vertically — eye-level comparison, no axis labels needed because the values are printed on each bar.
**Anti-example:** A pie chart of municipal-budget allocation at 5cm on a flyer panel — too small to read slices, requires a legend, not skimmable.

# Principles serving both axes

## 19. The grid is the backbone, not decoration

**Source:** Josef Müller-Brockmann, *Grid Systems in Graphic Design* (1981); Karl Gerstner, *Designing Programmes* (1964).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Every layout sits on an explicit grid (typically 6, 8, or 12 columns for a DIN-lang panel). Every element aligns to grid lines on both x and y axes. The grid is invisible to the reader but produces the perceived calm and order that distinguishes professional from amateur work.
**Worked example:** A falzflyer panel on an 8-column grid with 4mm gutters: portrait spans columns 1–4, headline columns 5–8, body columns 1–8 below. Every edge lines up.
**Anti-example:** Logo at (12mm, 8mm), portrait at (47mm, 22mm), headline at (60mm, 18mm) — coordinates pulled from nowhere, no element shares an edge with another, no grid relationships. The user described this as "just randomly placed" — that is exactly what off-grid composition feels like.

## 20. Apply Gestalt grouping deliberately (proximity > similarity > closure)

**Source:** Max Wertheimer, *Untersuchungen zur Lehre von der Gestalt* (1923); systematized in Kurt Koffka, *Principles of Gestalt Psychology* (1935); applied to design in Lupton & Phillips, *Graphic Design: The New Basics* (2008, ch. on Gestalt).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Group related items by proximity (move them closer than their distance to unrelated items); reinforce groups with similarity (same color, weight, or shape); avoid relying on closure (boxes, rules) when proximity alone suffices. Boxes and rules are a failure of proximity.
**Worked example:** Three theme cards with their titles + descriptions tightly stacked (3mm internal spacing) and 14mm gaps between cards. No boxes needed — proximity does the work.
**Anti-example:** Same three themes with equal 8mm spacing everywhere, then drawn boxes around each to "make them group" — the boxes are visual debt incurred because proximity wasn't used.

## 21. Figure-ground clarity, ruthlessly

**Source:** Edgar Rubin, *Synsoplevede Figurer* (1915), translated as *Visual Figures* (1921); reinforced in Donis A. Dondis, *A Primer of Visual Literacy* (1973).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** At every point on the page the eye must be able to decide instantly what is figure (foreground/content) and what is ground (background). Avoid placing text on busy photo areas, gradients that compete with content, or near-equal foreground/background luminance. When text must overlap an image, use a solid color block, a heavy darken/lighten, or move the text.
**Worked example:** Headline placed over an image where the image area behind the text has been darkened with a 45%-black gradient overlay — text reads cleanly, image still visible.
**Anti-example:** White headline placed directly over a photo with a sky-and-foliage background — half the headline is unreadable because the foliage and the type compete for the same luminance band.

## 22. Alignment is invisible glue

**Source:** Ellen Lupton, *Thinking with Type* (2nd ed., 2010, p. 113); also Robin Williams, *The Non-Designer's Design Book* (1994), the "CRAP" framework — Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity.
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Every element shares an edge or center-line with at least one other element. Mixed alignments (some left, some center, some right) without a structural reason produce visual stress.
**Worked example:** All headlines, body, and captions left-aligned to column 1 of the grid; only the page number is right-aligned. Two alignment systems max.
**Anti-example:** Logo center-aligned, headline left-aligned, body justified, caption right-aligned, footer center-aligned — five alignment systems on one panel, page feels like it is vibrating.

## 23. Discoverability — first-glance test in 3 seconds

**Source:** Don Norman, *The Design of Everyday Things* (1988, rev. 2013), on signifiers and discoverability — ported to print by Krug (2000).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Within 3 seconds of first glance the viewer must be able to answer: who is this from, what is the offer, when/where to act. If any of those three are unclear at 3 seconds, the design has failed regardless of how it looks. Test by showing a draft for 3 seconds and asking the three questions.
**Worked example:** First glance reveals: Grüne logo + candidate face (who) → "Bezahlbar wohnen" headline (what) → "Wahltag 10. Mai" + QR (when/where). Three answers, one panel.
**Anti-example:** The viewer sees a portrait and a headline but cannot find the party identity (logo too small, no green) or the action (no date, no CTA). The flyer reads as "a person," not "a Grüne candidate asking for a vote on a date."

## 24. Repetition creates rhythm and identity

**Source:** Robin Williams, *The Non-Designer's Design Book* (1994); also Massimo Vignelli on systems, *Vignelli Canon* (2008).
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** Repeat type styles, color blocks, and spacing units across panels of a multi-panel piece (falzflyer, brochure). Each panel should feel like a sibling of the others, not a stranger. Identical theme-card structure across all theme panels of a falzflyer is the strongest example.
**Worked example:** Every theme panel of a falzflyer has the same anatomy: green color bar at top (10mm), title in 24pt below, 4-line body in 11pt, statistic in 36pt, all in the same positions. Six panels, one design system.
**Anti-example:** Theme 1 has the title at top, theme 2 has it at bottom, theme 3 centers it. Reads as three drafts shoved together.

## 25. Brand-mark placement is non-negotiable, not creative

**Source:** Wally Olins, *On Brand* (2003); reinforced by every party CD-Quickguide including Grüne's own.
**Axis:** A + T
**Rule:** The party logo (Bund Grüne mark) lives in a fixed, predictable location across an entire piece — typically top-left or bottom-right, never floating. Size, clear-space, and color variant follow the CD-Quickguide rules without exception. Logo placement is a brand integrity decision, not a layout decision.
**Worked example:** Logo bottom-right of every outward-facing panel, fixed clear-space of 1× the logo height, always in Dunkelgrün on light backgrounds. Predictable identity anchor.
**Anti-example:** Logo upper-left on cover, upper-right on inside, missing on back — the user named this as "randomly placed" and it directly violates the brand guideline.

# Quick decision checklist

A one-page test to run on any draft. If a draft fails any of these, do not advance it.

**Three-second skim test (T)**
- [ ] Within 3 seconds, can a stranger identify: who (party + candidate), what (the message), when/where (the action)?

**Hierarchy and reading order (T)**
- [ ] Does the dominant element own ≥40% of optical weight?
- [ ] Is there a deliberate, traceable gaze path from entry to CTA?
- [ ] Is there a ≥2.5× size jump between headline and body?

**Type discipline (T + A)**
- [ ] ≤ 2 type families and ≤ 3 type sizes per panel?
- [ ] Body 10–11pt minimum; line length 45–75 characters?
- [ ] Body contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 (≥ 7:1 preferred)?

**Color discipline (A + T)**
- [ ] Primary color reads as Grüne (Hellgrün or Dunkelgrün dominant)?
- [ ] ≤ 2 non-green accent colors? No accidental SPD-red, AfD-blue, FDP-yellow, Linke-magenta?

**Grid and alignment (A + T)**
- [ ] Every element aligned to an explicit grid (no off-grid coordinates)?
- [ ] ≤ 2 alignment systems on the panel?
- [ ] Logo placement consistent across all panels of the piece, per CD-Quickguide?

**Composition (A)**
- [ ] ≥ 30% intentional negative space?
- [ ] Layout is asymmetric-balanced, not center-stacked?
- [ ] If a face is present: cropped tight (≥ 60% of frame), gaze directed into the layout?

**Gestalt and clarity (A + T)**
- [ ] Related items grouped by proximity, not by drawn boxes?
- [ ] Figure-ground clear at every point — no text fighting busy backgrounds?

**Anti-chartjunk (T)**
- [ ] Every graphic element carries information? Drop shadows, gradients, decorative rules removed unless functional?

**CTA (T)**
- [ ] Exactly one CTA per panel that has one?
- [ ] CTA placed near the natural exit point of the gaze path?

**Repetition across panels (A + T)**
- [ ] Theme/section panels share identical anatomy across the piece?
- [ ] Type styles, color blocks, and spacing units repeat?

**Brand integrity (A + T)**
- [ ] Logo size, clear-space, and color variant per CD-Quickguide?

If a draft passes all of these, it has cleared the floor. It still requires a human verdict on appeal and information transport before it ships.
